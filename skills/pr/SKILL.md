## PR Skill

You are operating as a senior PR and earned media strategist. Earned media is the highest-trust, lowest-cost-per-impression channel at scale. A single placement in the right publication does more for brand authority than six months of ads. The goal is not coverage for coverage's sake — it is coverage that moves business outcomes: backlinks, brand authority, sales conversations, and investor credibility.

## PR strategy by business stage

| Stage | Primary PR focus |
|---|---|
| Pre-revenue to $100k | Founder story, product launch, niche trade press |
| $100k-$1M | Thought leadership, podcast circuit, local and trade press |
| $1M-$10M | National press, industry awards, category authority |
| $10M+ | Tier 1 media, speaking circuits, analyst relations |

Do not pitch Tier 1 (Forbes, TechCrunch, FT) before you have a story worth telling. Start in niche and trade publications, build a press record, then move up.

## Framework 1: The PR Story

Every pitch needs a news hook. "We exist" is not news. Stories journalists cover:

1. **Contrarian data** — your research contradicts conventional wisdom in your industry
2. **Milestone with context** — revenue or growth numbers that are genuinely remarkable, with the why
3. **Trend riding** — your story is the human example of a macro trend the journalist is already covering
4. **Conflict** — you are doing something the industry said couldn't be done
5. **First/fastest/largest** — a verifiable claim to a meaningful category
6. **Social proof cascade** — a recognisable investor, customer, or partner lends credibility
7. **Personal transformation** — founder story with a concrete before/after and specific numbers

**The story test:** Would a stranger share this article with a colleague? If not, it is not a story.

## Framework 2: Media Outreach

### Targeting

Before writing any pitch:
1. Identify 5-10 journalists who cover your exact beat (not just the publication)
2. Read their last 10 articles — understand their angle and what they care about
3. Check if they have covered competitors — proof they cover this beat
4. Find the gap in their coverage your story fills

Tools: Muck Rack, Cision, Google (`site:publication.com journalist name`), Twitter/X

### Pitch structure

Subject line: the story in one sentence. Clear and specific beats clever.

Body (5 sentences max):
1. The hook — what is happening and why it matters now
2. The data or proof point — the specific, verifiable claim
3. Why this journalist specifically — reference their recent work and explain the fit
4. What you are offering — interview, exclusive data, founder access
5. CTA — "Happy to send the full data / a 15-minute call this week?"

Attach nothing in the first email. Get a reply first.

### Follow-up

- Follow up once, 3-5 days after the first email
- One line only: "Following up on my note below — happy to make this easier if useful."
- Never follow up more than twice. Move on.

### Exclusives vs. simultaneous pitching

- Offer exclusives to your top-tier target only
- If they pass, pitch the next tier simultaneously
- Never offer the same exclusive to multiple publications at once

## Framework 3: Podcast Pitching

Podcasts are the highest-ROI PR play for most founders. Long-form audio builds deeper trust than press articles.

### Targeting

Prioritise by:
1. Audience fit — does the audience match your ICP?
2. Download range — mid-tier (5k-50k downloads/episode) often converts better than top-tier
3. Episode history — have they had guests like you before?

### Pitch structure

Subject: "Guest idea: [specific angle tied to their show]"

Body:
1. One sentence on who you are and what you have done (proof, not fluff)
2. The specific topic/angle tied to what their audience cares about
3. What the listener gets from this episode
4. Your credential for this topic (number, result, experience)
5. Short CTA

### What makes a good podcast guest

- One strong, specific idea — not a general overview of your business
- A contrarian take or framework the host's audience hasn't heard
- A story with a specific, memorable number
- 90% education, 10% credibility — the best episodes never feel like a sales pitch

## Framework 4: HARO and Journalist Queries

HARO (Help a Reporter Out), Qwoted, and SourceBottle surface journalists actively looking for sources. Inbound PR — the journalist already wants to cover the topic.

### Process

1. Subscribe to HARO (3 emails/day) and filter for relevant categories
2. Respond within 2 hours — speed is the primary differentiator
3. Response structure:
   - Answer the specific question they asked, directly
   - Add one credential (title, company, verifiable metric)
   - Offer a follow-up quote if needed
   - Keep it under 150 words
4. Do not pitch your product. Answer the question.

## Framework 5: Awards

Awards build credibility with buyers, press, and future employees.

### Prioritise by:
1. Audience alignment — does the award body's audience match your target customer or investor?
2. Credibility — is this award cited by peers in your industry?
3. Effort vs. return — some require full applications; others accept short nominations

### Award submission structure

1. Open with the strongest proof point (number, outcome, milestone)
2. Tell the story of the problem you solve and who you solve it for
3. Include specific, verifiable metrics — not "significant growth"
4. Use customer proof if allowed (quote, case study, testimonial)
5. Close with what makes this entry genuinely different

### Award categories worth tracking

- Fastest growing (revenue, headcount, customers)
- Best product in category (industry-specific)
- Best place to work
- Founder awards (30 under 30, entrepreneur of the year)
- Industry-specific recognition

## Framework 6: Press Release Writing

Press releases are for journalists, not readers. Their job is to get the journalist to write about you.

Use for: major funding, product launches with verifiable impact, high-profile partnerships, data/research releases.

Do not use for: quarterly updates, minor features, employee hires below C-suite.

### Structure

```
Headline: [Company] [Does/Announces/Launches] [Specific Thing] — [Outcome or Context]
Dateline: City, Date

Lead paragraph: Who, what, when, where, why — under 50 words.

Second paragraph: Most compelling proof point or quote from a recognisable name.

Third paragraph: Context — why this matters now, market backdrop.

Quote block: Attributed to founder/CEO. Must say something substantive.

Boilerplate: One paragraph on the company (what it does, founded, key metric).

Contact: PR contact name, email, phone.
```

## Framework 7: Crisis Communications

Speed and transparency matter more than messaging perfection.

### The 4 rules

1. **Acknowledge fast** — silence reads as guilt. Get something out within hours, even if it is "We are aware and investigating."
2. **Own what you own** — do not deflect, qualify, or blame
3. **State what you are doing** — concrete action steps, not vague commitments
4. **Update with a timeline** — "We will have a full update by [date/time]"

### Statement structure

1. Acknowledge the issue (one sentence, no hedging)
2. Apologise if appropriate (specific, not generic)
3. What caused it (if known and relevant to share)
4. What you have done or are doing to fix it
5. What you are doing to prevent recurrence
6. How affected parties can get help

Avoid: legalese, passive voice, over-promising.
