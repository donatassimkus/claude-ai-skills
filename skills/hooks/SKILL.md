# Hooks Skill — How to Double Your Marketing in Under 5 Seconds

> **Scope:** this skill covers hook craft: types, structures, testing frameworks, and the Colleidoscope process. Channel strategy (paid media budgets, email infrastructure, content calendars) is a separate discipline and is out of scope here. Hook taxonomy is the layer every channel draws on.

## What a Hook Is

A hook is whatever gets the attention of your audience. It is the first thing they see, hear, or read.

- In a video: the first 2-5 seconds of footage or speech
- In an ad: the opening line, visual, or audio
- In an email: the subject line
- In a social post: the first line before "read more"
- In outreach: the opening sentence

**The hook does one job:** sell the person on paying attention to what comes next.

If no one notices your content, nothing else matters. The offer, the copy, the production quality — irrelevant if they scroll past. The hook is the gatekeeper to everything downstream.

---

## Why Hooks Are Worth Obsessing Over

A better hook on the same ad or piece of content can produce:
- 2x, 5x, or 10x more clicks, views, and leads
- Lower cost per lead (same spend, more engaged people)
- Access to colder audiences who would have ignored a weaker hook
- Higher scale ceiling (hooks that convert cold traffic let you raise budget)

The rest of the content, offer, and funnel stays the same. Only the hook changes. This is why good advertisers spend as much time on the first 5 seconds as everything else combined.

## How a Hook Works: Two Required Parts

Every hook has two parts. Both must be present.

### Part 1: The Call Out
Gets the right person to say "this is for me."

This can be verbal (naming the target: "business owners…", "if you're doing X…") or nonverbal (a visual or sound that signals relevance to a specific person or situation).

The cocktail party effect: people tune out almost everything in a noisy environment, but they snap to attention when they hear their own name — or anything that directly identifies them. A great call out creates that snap.

### Part 2: The Condition for Value
Promises a payoff if they keep watching, reading, or listening.

Either explicit: "If [target] [does this action], they will get [outcome]."
Or implicit: the visual or opening scene implies a payoff without spelling it out.

**The test:** Does the hook make the right person think "I want to know how this ends"?

## 7 Verbal Hook Types

These are the structural patterns that work across all formats (ads, content, email, social, outreach). Each type works in different contexts.

### 1. Labels
Name the target person directly. Creates instant identification.
- "Attention [target audience] — I have something for you."
- "If you're a [type of person], read this."

Best for: ads, email subject lines, social posts, outreach openers.

### 2. Questions (Yes Questions)
Pose a question the target will answer "yes" to. Creates agreement and momentum.
- "Would you pay [small amount] to get [big outcome]?"
- "Are you tired of [specific pain]?"
- "Do you ever feel like you're working hard but going nowhere?"

Best for: ads, email subject lines, video hooks.

### 3. Questions (Open Questions)
Invite curiosity. No yes/no answer — they have to keep going to find out.
- "Which would you rather be: [A] or [B]?"
- "What would you do if [scenario]?"
- "How long does it actually take to [outcome]?"

Best for: social content, video, email subject lines.

### 4. Conditionals
Set up a scenario or condition that leads to a result. Filters the audience by situation.
- "If you're doing [X] and still not seeing [Y], here's why."
- "If you want [outcome] without [the thing they hate], try this."

Best for: social posts, ads, video intros, email subject lines.

### 5. Commands
Tell the reader exactly what to do. Works because it filters strongly — only relevant people act.
- "Read this if you want [outcome]."
- "Watch this before you [mistake they're about to make]."

Best for: ads, email subject lines, social.

### 6. Statements
Provocative or counterintuitive claims that demand attention.
- "The smartest thing you can do right now is [unexpected thing]."
- "How to get ahead of [overwhelming majority] of [target audience]."
- "[Number] ways to [undesirable outcome] — stop doing these."

Best for: social posts, video titles, email subject lines, landing page headlines.

### 7. Lists / Steps
Specificity signals value. Numbers create curiosity about what's in the list.
- "[Number] things [target audience] does that keeps them stuck."
- "The [number]-step process I used to [outcome]."

Best for: video titles, email subject lines, landing pages, social content.

### Bonus: Narratives and Exclamations
- **Narratives:** Story-style hooks that drop you into the middle of something happening. "One day I was at [scene] and [unexpected thing happened]." Creates a loop the audience needs to close.
- **Exclamations:** Express strong emotion (surprise, frustration, excitement) to trigger curiosity. "Ahhh — this is the one thing nobody tells you about [topic]."

## Nonverbal Hooks

Words are only one channel. Visuals and sound are hooks too.

**Visual hooks:**
- Unexpected or incongruous image/scene (why is that there?)
- Pattern interrupt (different format, style, or shot from everything else on the feed)
- Demonstration of an outcome (seeing the result before the explanation)
- Reaction face / emotional expression

**Sound hooks:**
- Unexpected noise or music
- A strong vocal opener (tone, pace, or energy that breaks the scroll)
- Environmental context that creates intrigue

**Rule:** On platforms where you can combine verbal + nonverbal, use both. The best hooks layer multiple attention signals simultaneously.

## The 70-20-10 Hook Framework

> This 70-20-10 applies to creative variation within a channel (hook types and angles). The same ratio is also used for channel budget allocation in paid media. Both apply simultaneously at different levels.

Based on Google's resource allocation model. Allocate your hook-creation effort in three buckets.

### 70% — What Already Works (Core)
Replicate and remix your best-performing hooks. This stabilises performance.

- Identify your top 10% performing content, ads, or emails
- Extract the hook structure
- Create new content using those hook patterns
- Track everything in a spreadsheet: platform, hook text, performance metric, link

This guarantees a consistent baseline. Most of your effort should go here.

### 20% — Winner Adjacent (Emerging)
Model hooks from top-performers in other niches or adjacent platforms.

- Use platform tools or manual search to find high-performing content in your space
- Isolate the hook structure (not the content — the structure)
- Adapt it to your topic and audience

This is how you discover new winning patterns before they become mainstream.

### 10% — Untested Ideas (Experimental)
Try completely new hook formats, visuals, or angles.

- No expectation of winning — you're buying data
- If it wins: move it to the 70% bucket and repeat it
- If it loses: document it so you never repeat it

Rule: never abandon the 70% that works to experiment too broadly.

## How to Build a Hook Library

**First time:**
1. Find the top-performing content, ads, and emails in your category (yours and competitors')
2. Isolate only the hooks (first line, first 5 seconds, subject line)
3. Write down your favourite 50
4. Test all of them in your own content and ads
5. Track performance

**Ongoing:**
1. Review the top 10% performers (pick 5)
2. Allocate 70% of new output to those 5 hook patterns
3. Allocate 20% to permutations and adjacent hooks
4. Allocate 10% to new experiments
5. Update the library continuously — remove what stops working, add new winners

**Format for your hook tracker spreadsheet:**
- Column: Platform
- Column: Hook text
- Column: Performance metric (views, CTR, reply rate, etc.)
- Column: Link
- One tab per platform

## Testing Protocol by Format

**Ads:** Record 10 hook variations for every 1 piece of ad content. Same offer, different openings. Run all 10 and let data determine the winner. Scale what wins.

**Short video content:** Take top-performing written hooks (tweets, posts) and record them as video openings. The hook dictates the video — not the other way around.

**Long video content:** Record 3-4 different openings in post-production. Pick the strongest before publishing.

**Email:** Run active split tests on every send. Document subject line winners in a separate file. Build the winning subject line library over time.

**Social posts:** The first line is the hook. Everything after the "read more" cutoff is the body. Test different first lines on the same content angle.

## Good Hook Checklist

Before publishing any piece of content, ad, email, or outreach — run this check:

**Call out:**
- [ ] Does the hook name or imply the specific type of person it's for?
- [ ] Will the right person immediately feel "this is about me"?

**Condition for value:**
- [ ] Does the hook imply a payoff? Will they get something if they keep going?
- [ ] Is the payoff worth their time (from their perspective, not yours)?

**Pattern interrupt:**
- [ ] Does it stand out from the other content around it?
- [ ] Is there anything unexpected, provocative, or curiosity-triggering?

**Specificity:**
- [ ] Is it specific enough to feel real and credible?
- [ ] Vague hooks underperform — add a number, name, situation, or detail.

## What Kills a Hook

**1. Leading with you instead of them.**
"I'm so excited to share my new [thing]" → nobody cares about you yet. Start with them.

**2. Vague promises.**
"In this video you'll learn something incredible" → means nothing. Be specific.

**3. Weak or absent call out.**
If it's for everyone, it's for no one. Name the person.

**4. Burying the hook.**
Warm-up sentences before the actual hook kill attention. Start with the hook, not the context.

**5. Ego-driven opening.**
Introducing yourself, your credentials, or your company before you've earned attention. Lead with value, not resume.

**6. Treating the hook as an afterthought.**
Writing the content first, then adding a hook. The hook dictates what content gets made — not the reverse.

## Hook Principles Summary

1. If nobody stops, nothing works. The hook is the only thing that matters first.
2. The hook sells attention. The content sells belief. The offer sells action.
3. Good hooks call out the right person AND promise value if they stay.
4. Specificity beats vagueness every time — numbers, names, situations.
5. Test more hooks than you think necessary. 10 variations per ad is not excessive.
6. The data teaches you what works. Let it.
7. Keep a hook library. Winners compound.
8. 70% repeat winners. 20% adjacent models. 10% experiments. Always.

## Squeezing a Winner: The Colleidoscope Process

Once a hook performs, do not abandon it. Squeeze it for 10x–100x output before creating anything new. Most advertisers move on too fast.

**Ways to multiply a winning hook:**
- Change backdrop, setting, or background color
- Swap props in the frame
- Re-record the same script in a different outfit
- Reorder the sequence (put the hook last, test a different opening)
- Apply visual filters (black and white, high contrast, sepia)
- Add, change, or remove on-screen captions, fonts, or text overlays
- Change pacing or playback speed
- Add or remove background music
- Use the same hook audio with different b-roll or visual

Each variation produces a new testable creative. Same proven message, different delivery. The algorithm treats these as new assets and finds different audiences for them.

**Why it works:** The audience has not seen every variation. The algorithm distributes different executions to different users. You are not repeating — you are amplifying a proven signal.

---

## Hook Session Structure: 40/10 Split

For every hook-writing or recording session:

- **40 hooks (80%):** Carbon copies or minor variations of proven performers. Same pain point, different wording, different angle, different background. Stays grounded in what already works.
- **10 hooks (20%):** Experimental — new formats, new awareness levels, new angles never tested.

This ratio keeps output grounded in proven performance while maintaining a test pipeline. Pure experimentation with no proven base wastes production budget. Pure repetition with no testing misses new audiences and awareness levels.

## Call-Out Specificity Principle

Call-outs run from hyper-specific to broad:

- Hyper-specific: "If you own a service business doing over 50 jobs per week with a team of 3 or more..."
- Specific: "If you're a service business owner struggling to generate consistent leads..."
- Broad: "If you want to grow your business..."

**Counter-intuitive finding:** More specific call-outs attract fewer total responses but far more qualified ones. Apparent CPL is higher; actual cost per qualified lead and cost per conversion are often lower.

**Research tactic:** Study competitor ads running unchanged for 90+ days. Long-running ads are profitable ads. Their call-outs reveal which specific audiences respond and convert for that offer category.

## Scoring a batch of hooks

To audit a set of existing hooks rather than write new ones, score every hook in scope against the Good Hook Checklist above (call-out, condition-for-value, pattern-interrupt, specificity). Each check = pass (1) or fail (0). Denominator = <M hooks> × 4 checks. Report as "<M> hooks × 4 checks from the Good Hook Checklist; <pass>/<total> pass".
