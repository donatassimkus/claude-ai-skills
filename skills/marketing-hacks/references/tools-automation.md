### 8. AI Chatbot Prompt Injection Risk [source](https://www.instagram.com/p/C1ePdbji33q/) · Dec 2023

`ai-security`, `chatbots`, `tools-workflows`

**What it does:** Businesses using AI chatbots (like GPT) on their websites are vulnerable to prompt injection attacks where users override the bot's instructions to extract absurd commitments.

**How to execute:**
1. If you deploy an AI chatbot on your site, add guardrails that prevent the bot from making pricing commitments or legally binding statements
2. Use system-level instructions that cannot be overridden by user input
3. Add disclaimers that chatbot responses do not constitute binding offers
4. Test your own chatbot by attempting prompt injection before launching

**Why it works:** Unprotected AI chatbots can be manipulated into making statements that damage brand trust or create PR incidents, as demonstrated by the Chevrolet dealership case.

### 126. Verify AI data outputs [source](https://www.instagram.com/p/CtKJ6UwJ6lk/) · Jun 2023

`tools-workflows`, `analytics`

**What it does:** Always manually cross-check quantitative data that ChatGPT produces before acting on it, because LLMs frequently hallucinate counts and statistics.

**How to execute:**
1. Ask ChatGPT to analyze or count data from your dataset (e.g., URL parameter frequency)
2. Before using the output, manually spot-check the numbers against the raw data
3. Use a second tool like Google Sheets AI or pivot tables to independently verify the same count
4. Compare both results; if they diverge, trust the spreadsheet or manual count over the LLM
5. Only act on data that has been confirmed by at least two sources

**Why it works:** LLMs are unreliable at precise counting and arithmetic, so decisions based on unverified AI output can lead you to optimize for the wrong things.
