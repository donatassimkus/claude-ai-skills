# 19 Ad Framework Archetypes

Production blueprints for high-performing ads. Each framework is a reusable structure. Swap the specifics for your product, audience, and offer. These complement the 5 ad format categories (Demonstration, Testimonial, Education, Story, Faceless) in the main skill. Formats describe what the ad is. Frameworks describe how to build it.

---

## Section 1: Testimonial and Proof Frameworks

### Framework 1: Personal Testimonial / Origin Story

**When to use:** You have a dramatic before/after story. Works for founders, customers, or employees.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** Make a wild claim or show an unexpected result in the first 5 seconds. No details yet.
2. **Anti-proof tour:** Walk through every reason the outcome should not have happened. Show the terrible circumstances. Each objection your prospect normally raises gets addressed as "anti-proof" (wrong location, no budget, bad timing, ugly setup, etc.).
3. **Open loop:** Stack more negatives. Do not resolve. Let curiosity build.
4. **Snap the loop:** Reveal overwhelming proof (receipts, dashboard, signed contracts, packed calendar). One plain sentence connects the dots.
5. **CTA:** Tie the lesson to the prospect's own limiting belief. Invite the next step.

**Production notes:**
- Shaky selfie style works. Social-native feel outperforms polished.
- Pan the camera to show the environment. Movement matters.
- Curiosity-driven from start to finish.

---

### Framework 3: Emotional Avatar Testimonial

**When to use:** A customer's story is genuinely moving. Best ad type for long-term performance. One strong testimonial can generate 100+ winning variations through the Kaleidoscope.

**6-Point Testimonial Script:**
1. What was life like before, personally?
2. What was life like before, professionally?
3. What skepticism did you have?
4. Why did you choose to take action anyway?
5. What is life like after, personally?
6. What is life like after, professionally?

**Narrative rhythm:** Personal stakes → Personal pain → Professional pain → Turning point → Work phase → Professional improvement → Personal improvement → Damaging admission → Reason why → CTA.

**Production notes:**
- Film the customer in a setting their peers would recognise. Their background signals "this is for people like me."
- Cut to an empty/sad version of the same setting within half a second of the opening.
- Emotional music underneath. Switch to upbeat at the turning point.
- Include one damaging admission ("it's not easy, but it's worth it") to increase credibility.
- End with a clear on-screen CTA plus voice-over.

---

### Framework 4: Show Don't Tell

**When to use:** Your outcome is visual. Packed venues, full calendars, before/after transformations, product in use, lines out the door.

**Structure:**
1. Show the dream outcome happening. No narration needed.
2. Frame what they see with minimal text (one headline, one number).
3. Visual CTA at the end (no spoken CTA required).

**Production notes:**
- Ask customers to film their "dream outcome proof moment."
- People moving in unison stops the scroll better than anything else. Synchronized action, groups working together, lines of people. The only thing better than many people moving is many people moving at the same time.
- Non-verbal ads get more organic reach as a bonus.
- Use bold, high contrast colour for any text overlay.
- Proof does the talking. If you need narration for viewers to understand the result, the ad will be weaker.

---

### Framework 5: Long Term Result

**When to use:** You want to attract advanced, high value buyers willing to pay more and stay longer. Optimise for LTV:CAC, not CAC.

**Structure:**
1. **Visual:** Customer in their workspace + bold headline with before/after metrics and a time range.
2. **Hook:** One sentence of pain only the right prospect would relate to.
3. **Pain stack:** 2-3 more specific pains using numbers and details advanced buyers would recognise (churn rate, margin, payback period).
4. **Decision point:** Name the solution and when they started. Include a date for credibility.
5. **Two wins:** Show a fast win (within weeks/months) and a big win (longer timeframe). Both matter.
6. **Soft metrics:** Add life improvements (family time, team growth, personal freedom). Covers both analytical and emotional buyer types.
7. **CTA:** Peer endorsement works better than a direct sell for advanced audiences.

**Key insight:** These ads cost more per lead but produce buyers who pay more, stay longer, get better results, and refer others. Do not kill this type of campaign based on CPL alone.

## Section 2: Education and Authority Frameworks

### Framework 6: Looking For 5 Avatars

**When to use:** Launching something new, first paid ads, or monetising an audience for the first time. One of the oldest direct response formats. Works in every niche.

**Template:** "I'm looking for [number] [avatars] who want to [dream outcome] but don't want to [biggest pain]. Here's why: [proof]. If interested, [next step]."

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** The template above. Be specific about who and what.
2. **Reason why limited spots:** "To get case studies" / "to prove it works" / "because we're new." Make it believable.
3. **Why your approach is different:** One sentence on how you are different from alternatives.
4. **Risk reversal:** Split risk with the customer. Performance element in compensation decreases risk.
5. **CTA:** "Click, apply, my team will qualify you." Mentioning an assistant reinforces authority.

**Production notes:**
- Educational setting (whiteboard, screen) for complex or high-ticket products.
- Selfie style for simple or mass-market products.
- Works great for organic audiences too. Mention who you are not looking for to increase credibility.

### Framework 7: New vs Old (ADucational)

**When to use:** You have a new mechanism, method, or model. Works especially well for complex or high-ticket products. Builds brand and drives sales simultaneously.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** Call out avatar + make a big claim with specific numbers. One breath.
2. **Old way:** Explain their current model. Describe the pain it causes. Be specific.
3. **Reveal the flaw:** Show why their current model structurally cannot scale.
4. **New way:** Name your mechanism (give it a non-obvious name to preserve curiosity). Explain how it inverts every problem listed in the old way.
5. **Math / hypothetical:** Run the numbers or walk through a scenario so the claim feels believable.
6. **Proof:** One authority line (revenue, customers served, years).
7. **CTA:** Preview the process: "Click, watch a short video, then apply for a call."

**Production notes:**
- Whiteboard or visual aid. Draw the old model, then erase or flip to the new.
- These turn you into a category authority over time.
- The more new/unknown you are, the more educational ads outperform direct response.

---

### Framework 8: Why You Will Never

**When to use:** You know your prospect's problems in detail. The more specific you are, the more they believe you can fix it.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** Call out + "X warning signs you will never reach [outcome]."
2. **Authority:** One proof line (companies helped, revenue generated).
3. **Rapid-fire problems:** List 3-5 specific, measurable problems your prospect is likely experiencing. Each one builds pressure. Use numbers, metrics, and scenarios only someone who truly knows the space would mention.
4. **Resolution:** Introduce your solution by name. One sentence on how it fixes everything above.
5. **CTA:** Opt in for a longer explainer (video sales letter, demo, lead magnet), then apply.

**Key insight:** You demonstrate authority by how specifically you can describe the problem. A problem so specific someone thinks "they must be an insider to know this." The specificity is the proof.

---

### Framework 9: North Star Metric

**When to use:** One single metric predicts your prospect's growth or failure. Map that metric to revenue tiers so they self-identify.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** Name the metric and its consequence. "If your [metric] is below [threshold], you will never reach [outcome]."
2. **Revenue ladder:** "Below X = stuck at [level]. At Y = you can reach [level]. At Z = [big outcome] becomes realistic." The prospect finds themselves in the hierarchy.
3. **Pain at each level:** Describe what they experience at their current metric level.
4. **Solution:** Your method moves the north star metric. Name it.
5. **CTA:** Opt in to see the full breakdown.

**Production notes:**
- Whiteboard with the metric ladder drawn out.
- Reuse the same framework for different metrics within the same niche. Many permutations possible.

---

### Framework 10: No BS Selfie

**When to use:** Quick, high-volume ad production. Top of funnel. Sells the lead magnet, not the product.

**Structure:**
1. **If-then pain hook:** "If you can't [metric], you're never going to [outcome]."
2. **One-sentence solution:** "I can show you how using [mechanism]."
3. **One proof line:** "[N] clients have done it."
4. **CTA:** "Swipe up / click the link."

**Production notes:**
- Walk and talk. Movement toward camera. Selfie style.
- Let the setting do the selling. The background communicates credibility (office, nice location, professional setting).
- Ultra-short. Constraining time forces only the words that matter.
- These work best when the landing page or funnel does the heavy conversion lifting.

## Section 3: Engagement and Entertainment Frameworks

### Framework 2: Prop Comedy

**When to use:** You want entertaining, fast-paced ads that filter for the right prospect.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** Call out the avatar. "Are you a [type]?"
2. **Value equation rapid-fire:** 3-4 "Yes" prompts hitting dream outcome, speed, ease, and proof. Each line is 3-5 seconds max.
3. **CTA:** One clear next step.

**Production notes:**
- One prop + one mini-setting per line. Match the first setting to the avatar's world.
- Film each line as a separate clip. Keep talent centred and moving.
- Whip-cuts or camera swings between clips. Momentum drags viewers forward.
- Upbeat royalty-free music + large, high-contrast captions.
- Freeze-frame the last CTA card for a full second. Bonus: screenshare the page they will go to.

---

### Framework 11: Trending Format

**When to use:** A platform trend (audio, meme, visual format) is hot. Ride it while it lasts.

**Structure:**
1. Use the trending format as the visual/audio framework.
2. Adapt your message to fit the trend's structure.
3. Make the content relevant to your avatar. The trend gets attention. Your message does the filtering.

**Production notes:**
- Speed matters. Trends have a short window. Ship fast.
- These can feel "unserious" but often outperform polished ads in reach and engagement.
- Works especially well for broad awareness and audience growth.

---

### Framework 14: Underdog

**When to use:** You are new, small, or have an obvious weakness. Own it.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** Lead with your biggest weakness or damaging admission.
2. **Stack negatives:** List every reason you should not succeed.
3. **Flip:** Reveal proof of results despite the disadvantages.
4. **CTA:** "If we can do it with [disadvantage], imagine what happens when you [take action]."

**Production notes:**
- Authenticity is the mechanism. Do not polish it.
- These build extreme trust because you lead with what everyone else hides.

## Section 4: Offer and Curiosity Frameworks

### Framework 12: Value Anchor

**When to use:** You are giving away something free or at low cost. Anchor the perceived value before revealing the price.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** State the total value of what they get. Use a specific number.
2. **Stack:** Walk through each component and its standalone value.
3. **Reveal:** "And it's free" / "And it costs [fraction of the anchored value]."
4. **Proof:** Show user counts, testimonials, or screenshots.
5. **CTA:** Show the signup flow (screenshare the page they'll land on).

**Production notes:**
- Internal animation or screen recording of the actual product/landing page reduces friction.
- If you can show what happens after they click, do it. Reducing uncertainty about the next step removes the final barrier.

---

### Framework 13: Bribe

**When to use:** Lead capture. You want emails, registrations, or opt-ins fast.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** "Real quick, what's your [contact info]? The reason I ask is..."
2. **Bribe:** Promise to send something overwhelmingly valuable.
3. **Value proof:** Explain your investment in creating it (hours, money, iterations).
4. **Mystery:** Tease an additional unnamed bonus. Give a range: "more than [low item] but less than [high item]." People fill in the blank with their best-case scenario.
5. **CTA:** "Register / opt in."

**Production notes:**
- Point directly at viewer. Pattern interrupt.
- The vaguer the mystery bonus, the better. You cannot beat someone's imagination.
- Once you have a winning version, convert it into static image variants (Kaleidoscope).

---

### Framework 15: Challenge With Prize

**When to use:** Product-led or community-led growth. You want users to take a specific action.

**Structure:**
1. **Give away something insane.** Find the most valuable thing your audience wants that you can afford to give.
2. **Time constraint.** "In the next 30 days" / "this month only."
3. **Social proof:** Reference a past winner who got the prize.
4. **One clear metric to win.** Most referrals, highest score, best transformation. Simple beats complex.
5. **Show how you help them win.** Promise tools, checklists, weekly tips. Hand-holding overcomes "I'll get stuck."
6. **Make losing valuable too.** Even if they miss the grand prize, they walk away with something (skills, discounts, the community itself).

---

### Framework 16: Flying Prop

**When to use:** Curiosity-driven launch or event promotion. Pure attention play.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** Catch a flying object while looking at camera.
2. **Joke tie-in:** One-liner connecting the prop to your offer.
3. **Stack reasons:** Mention time, money, and effort invested. Approximates value for the prospect.
4. **Mystery:** Tease an unnamed bonus. Leave the loop open. Only taking action closes it.
5. **CTA:** Register / opt in.

**Production notes:**
- Real objects moving always beat digital motion.
- Daisy-chain open loops: prop → investment → secret → action.
- Keep a prop closet. Anything that catches your eye in daily life will catch someone else's on screen.

---

### Framework 17: Rumors Are True

**When to use:** Product launch or event with pre-existing buzz (or manufactured buzz).

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** "The rumors are true..." (leverages FOMO and social proof)
2. **What they get:** Frame it as over-the-top free. Iceberg metaphor: show the visible part, hint at more.
3. **Investment proof:** Hours, money, iterations invested. Show receipts if possible.
4. **Mystery:** Tease additional surprises.
5. **CTA:** Visual only (end screen) or spoken. Test both.

**Production notes:**
- Make multiple versions in multiple formats immediately. Static image versions of winning video hooks also tend to win.
- The hook "The rumors are true" works in any market. It triggers two responses: feeling left out, and feeling left out of something important.

---

### Framework 18: You're Being Lied To

**When to use:** Old way vs new way. Confrontational tone. Works when your audience is frustrated with the status quo.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** Walk toward camera aggressively. "You're being lied to."
2. **The lie:** Name the old belief ("if you build it, they will come").
3. **The truth:** Reframe with your new way ("if you tell them, they will find you").
4. **Proof:** Revenue, exits, portfolio numbers.
5. **Selfish reason why:** Share your bias openly. People trust people who admit their motives.
6. **Speed reframe:** Explain how much time you compress for them. "A decade in a few hours."
7. **CTA:** Flash large text, tear away to reveal a button. "Tap to learn the new way."

**Production notes:**
- Walking and talking. Movement toward camera. Confrontational energy.
- Ads where the talent moves almost always outperform static delivery.
- This pairs well with a visual tearaway (poster, whiteboard) revealing the CTA.

---

### Framework 19: Data Stack

**When to use:** Your product or service has impressive stats. Numbers at the start of ads tend to outperform.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook:** "This is going to blow your mind."
2. **Stat rapid-fire:** List impressive numbers about your product: words written, hours invested, customers served, results delivered. Speed through them.
3. **Social proof:** "[N] people have already [taken action]."
4. **Scarcity:** Explain the genuine limitation (limited hours, limited inventory, limited seats).
5. **CTA:** "Register / buy / opt in before [deadline]."

**Production notes:**
- Half-written board = open loop. People want to see it completed.
- Collect all your impressive stats before the recording session. Select the top 5-7.
- Numbers work as hooks across every niche and audience type.

---

## How to Use These Frameworks

1. Pick 3-5 frameworks matching your current assets (do you have testimonials? proof shots? a new mechanism?).
2. Record one version of each.
3. Test all of them.
4. When a winner emerges, run it through the Ad Kaleidoscope (see main skill file).
5. 80% of effort on Kaleidoscope variations of the winner. 20% testing new frameworks.
6. Repeat.

Every framework works across industries. Swap the details, keep the structure. If you find yourself thinking "this won't work for me," reframe to "how can I make this work for me."
